PAEI Management Style Indicator

The Four Management Styles

P

Producer (P) — The Driver

Short-term results · Execution · Action orientation

The Producer role is focused on getting things done. Producers are highly results-oriented, motivated by short-term goals, and thrive on action. They know what needs to be accomplished and push hard to deliver it — on time, within budget, and to the required standard.

In organisations, the P function provides the energy and drive to move from planning to execution. Without adequate P function, organisations talk without doing — paralysed by discussion while opportunities pass.

At their best, Producers are decisive, persistent, and intensely focused. They cut through ambiguity and deliver. At their worst, they become Lone Rangers — doing everything themselves, burning out, and failing to develop others around them.

A

Administrator (A) — The Organiser

Systems · Process · Reliability · Order

The Administrator role is focused on how things are done. Administrators build systems, establish processes, and ensure that the organisation functions efficiently, consistently, and in compliance with its own standards. They are the guardians of institutional memory and procedural discipline.

Without adequate A function, organisations become chaotic — innovation and energy dissipate into disorder. With too much A and too little of other functions, organisations become rigid and bureaucratic, optimising the process at the expense of results.

At their best, Administrators are thorough, reliable, and precise — ensuring that what works today still works tomorrow. At their worst, they become Bureaucrats — following rules that no longer serve the organisation's goals, prioritising procedure over purpose.

E

Entrepreneur (E) — The Visionary

Change · Innovation · Long-term vision · Risk-taking

The Entrepreneur role is focused on what could be done. Entrepreneurs are driven by change, innovation, and the future. They identify opportunities before others see them, challenge the status quo, and inspire organisations to evolve. They are energised by possibilities and have a high tolerance for risk.

Without adequate E function, organisations stagnate — incrementally optimising a declining strategy. With too much E and insufficient A and P, organisations launch initiatives that never land and exhaust their people through constant change.

At their best, Entrepreneurs see the future clearly and inspire others to build it. At their worst, they become Arsonists — burning down what works in pursuit of the next exciting idea, leaving trails of unfinished projects.

I

Integrator (I) — The Cohesive Shepherd

People · Consensus · Team harmony · Long-term cohesion

The Integrator role is focused on who needs to be aligned. Integrators build team cohesion, resolve interpersonal conflicts, and ensure that the organisation functions as a united whole rather than a collection of competing silos. They are skilled listeners, consensus builders, and political navigators.

Without adequate I function, organisations fracture — talented individuals pull in different directions, knowledge is hoarded, and politics undermine execution. With too much I and insufficient P and E, organisations drift toward groupthink and conflict-avoidance.

At their best, Integrators create environments where diverse talent thrives and the whole exceeds the sum of its parts. At their worst, they become Super-Followers — unable to make unpopular decisions, prioritising harmony over necessary change.
