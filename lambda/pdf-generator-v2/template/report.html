Getting the Most from Your LEAP Report

LEAP is a leadership development tool designed to increase self-awareness — not to place you in a box or label your leadership style. Rather than measuring personality, LEAP compares three perspectives of your leadership:

Current State — How you are leading today.
Role Expectations — The leadership energy your current role requires.
Natural Preference — The leadership energy that comes most naturally to you.

Leadership effectiveness is not about expressing one element more than another. Different roles require different combinations of leadership energy. The most effective leaders understand their natural strengths, recognize when adaptation is needed, and intentionally develop the flexibility to meet the demands of the situation.

As you progress through this report, you'll discover where these three perspectives are aligned, where they differ, and the practical actions that can help you lead with greater effectiveness, authenticity, and sustainable energy.

Clarifying the Three Perspectives

Current State, Role Expectations, and Natural Preference. These three perspectives were introduced on the previous page. Here, they are brought together to help you compare how your leadership energy shows up across your current behaviour, your role's demands, and what comes most naturally to you.

There are no "good" or "bad" profiles. Different roles require different combinations of leadership energy. The goal is not to maximize any one dimension, but to understand where your leadership energy is aligned and where conscious adaptation may be required.

Understanding the Three Gaps

Your report compares these three perspectives in three important ways:

Execution Gap
Role Expectations ↔ Current State
Shows how closely your current behaviour aligns with what your role requires.
Engagement Gap
Role Expectations ↔ Natural Preference
Shows how naturally your role draws upon the leadership energy that motivates you.
Authenticity Gap
Current State ↔ Natural Preference
Shows how much adaptation you are making between how you naturally prefer to lead and how you are currently leading.

Gaps are not inherently good or bad. What matters is understanding where they support your effectiveness and where they may begin to drain your energy over time.

The following page explores your three most significant gaps in detail and explains what they may mean in your day-to-day leadership.
